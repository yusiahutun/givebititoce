    If you're counting the days until season two of "Grace and Frankie," well, you're in luck. Ripcord, a new comedy by David Lindsay-Abaire and directed by David Hyde Pierce, operates as what could be a sequel to the popular Netflix series: It's a wily, witty take on two senior-age women getting to know each other as roommates in an assisted-living facility that shows being fabulous at any age comes down to a little mischief.

    Media Platforms Design Team

    When the play first starts we meet Abby (Holland Taylor, whom you may recall last from her show Ann featured here, sitting alone in a chair scrolling her iPad. Her half of the room is sparse and spotless, while the other half is unkempt and a hodge-podge of tchotchkes arranged on a shelf. Her roommate is Marilyn (Marylouise Burke), a dingy, loud woman who sees the silver lining a bit too often for Abby. What ensues after is the result of a playful bet the women forge to pass the time: Marilyn claims she can genuinely scare tough gal Abby, and Abby says she can make happy-go-lucky Marilyn angry. The prize? The bed by the window. At a certain age, it seems, the simple things in life, whether part of your past or present, become the most treasured and sometimes you just have to let 'er "rip" to find out.

    To say the show is perfectly cast (Rachel Dratch plays Marilyn's daughter Colleen ta-boot) would likely take the Tony for understatement of the year. And the writing, which drives the comedic effect of making light of growing old and getting on, is sharp: case in point, Abby's love of profanity and obsession with her iPad.     Ripcord is open through December 6 at the Manhattan Theatre Club at City Center.

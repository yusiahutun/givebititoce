    For those considering adopting children, it is prudent to have a clear understanding of the process in order to have a smooth experience.

    Child adoption procedure

    This article gives a step-by-step procedure, requirements and what the law says about children adoption.

    Speaking to TUKO.co.ke, revered lawyer Julius Miiri said the adoption of children in Kenya is governed by the Constitution, the Children’s Act 2001 and the guidelines for alternative family care of Children in Kenya.

    In deciding any case involving a child, courts are obliged to prioritise the child's best interest.

    The rigorous adoption process ensures that the adoptive parent is capable of taking care of the child.

    The adoption application is filed in any High Court in Kenya.

    Requirements

    Miiri told this publication that the law provides no adoption shall commence unless the child is at least six weeks old and less than 18 years.

    Where the child is abandoned then he/she must be declared free for adoption by a registered adoption society.

    The Kenya Children Home Adoption Society must provide a document certifying that the child is free for adoption.

    If the child is above 14 years, they need to give their consent to be adopted.

    Also, there must be consent from the parent/guardian accepting the child to be adopted.

    However, the court may waive the consent where, for example, the parent/guardian is unknown and untraceable, has persistently mistreated the child, neglected the child, or fails to attend the adoption court proceedings.

    Further, the applicant or the person intending to adopt must have attained the age of 25 and is at least 21 years older than the child but has not attained the age of 65 years.

    The applicant must also prove to the High Court that they can cater to the child's needs.

    Adoption cannot be issued to:

  • A sole male applicant where the child is female.
  • A sole female applicant where the child is male.
  • Applicants who have attained the age of 65 years old.
  • A sole foreign parent.

    • However, an adoption under these categories of applicants may only be allowed where the court is satisfied that there are special circumstances to allow it.

    Someone of unsound mind under the Mental Health Act, a convict of any sexual offence, immoral behaviour, and unnatural offences, a gay person and unmarried joint applicants are totally barred from adoption.

    Procedure

    Once an adoption hopeful is ready to start the process, the parents of the child to be adopted should place the child at the disposal of a registered adoption society.

    The parents then sign an explanatory memorandum and the society takes the child in.

    If the child is already under the care of an adoption society, the applicant does not need to trace the child's parents.

    For abandoned children, one must also provide the court with a letter from a police station stating that the child has not been claimed and a Committal Order committing the child to a children's home.

    The applicant will then forward their application to the adoption society, which allocates a social worker to the case after receiving it.

    The social worker then makes an appointment to visit the applicant’s home to determine their environment's suitability for the child.

    The social worker, in turn, makes a report of their assessment which will be used to either approve or deny the adoption application.

    Before making the judgement, the court will consider the application and the social worker's report.

    Upon being satisfied that the child is free for adoption and the adopter is fit to adopt, Miiri said the court will then issue the Adoption Order.

    The registrar will then register the adoption and issue a certificate of adoption to the applicant, which is usually within seven days.

    In terms of payments, the registrar might only charge you the registration fees, which is about KSh 100.

    Documents needed in adoption process

  • The child’s birth certificate.
  • Chief’s letter.
  • If the child is a school-going child, a copy of the school progress report.
  • A children officers report.
  • Death certificate for deceased parents of the child.
  • Certificates of good conduct.
  • Copies of identification documentation of prospective adoptive parents.
  • Marriage certificate for the couple wishing to adopt.
  • Medical report of the adoptive parent.
  • Proof of financial status such as bank statements and payslips.
  • Proof of home ownership.
  • Birth certificates of any children the adoptive parent may have.

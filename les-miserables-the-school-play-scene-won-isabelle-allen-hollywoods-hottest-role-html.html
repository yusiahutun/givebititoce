    With her pale skin, golden hair and haunting blue eyes, ten-year-old Isabelle Allen has a face that can break hearts. It’s a ghostly, waiflike look that made her a natural choice to play Cosette, as a child, in the much-vaunted £62million film version of Les Miserables, and which now seems likely to make her a star.

    But as these never-before-seen pictures reveal, it is an image that is very much Isabelle’s own. The photographs were taken a year ago when she played a ragamuffin in an amateur school pro- duction of The Pied Piper of Hamelin. 

    Although she is portraying a lame little boy, supported by a tiny crutch, she is instantly recognisable as the girl whose face now adorns Les Miserables film posters everywhere.

    Unknown: Isabelle Allen playing a ragamuffin in The Pied Piper in an amateur school production. She is instantly recognisable as the girl whose face now adorns Les Miserables film posters everywhere

    Jeremy James Taylor, founder of the National Youth Music Theatre, says  Isabelle’s star potential was obvious  from the moment he met her at the Moira House school production in Eastbourne. ‘From the moment she auditioned I knew that she was Cosette,’ said Taylor, speaking from Australia where he is now working.

    ‘I was auditioning pupils when in walked this little waif. I thought, “I know your face.” I couldn’t think from where, but when she opened her mouth and sang, I realised she was the image of the little girl from the Les Miserables stage poster. She looked exactly like the original iconic drawing.

    ‘I knew they were casting for the film because a lot of my mates are in it. And I had been thinking a little bit about the young ones who would be taking part. I always keep an eye open for new talent.

    ‘I told Isabelle’s parents there was  a good chance she could be cast as Cosette. They were stunned. I don’t think they had any inkling she could become a professional actress.

    Walking tall: Isabelle on the red carpet for the film premiere at the Odeon Leicester Square on Wednesday night

    On set: Isabelle with Anne Hathaway who plays Fantine

    ‘I made a few calls and managed to get someone to see her. The rest, as they say, is history.’

    Taylor went on: ‘I have seen many young talents over the years and  Isabelle has that something extra.  You can call it charisma or screen presence; she’s got it.

    ‘She did brilliantly in the school production and when I see her face on the film posters now I feel so proud.

    ‘I’ve heard she’s absolutely marvellous in the film. I’m not surprised.  She is a natural – she has a face the camera loves, sings beautifully and can also act.’

    One year after Taylor spotted her,  Isabelle now stars alongside A-list actors Hugh Jackman, Russell Crowe and Anne Hathaway.

    Jackman describes Isabelle as the heart of the film. ‘It’s her first movie and she has no idea how good she is,’ he said. ‘She’s incredible. Totally pure and unspoilt. She is the movie.’

    Taylor, who was awarded an OBE for services to musical theatre and young people in 2010, works extensively in schools as a visiting director. Isabelle is the latest in a long line of successful talent-spotting.

    In the 37 years since he founded the NYMT he has helped shape some of Britain’s leading performers, including actor Eddie Redmayne, who plays the student revolutionary Marius in Les Mis. He also spied the potential  in Jamie Bell, Tom Chambers and Sheridan Smith.

    ‘I look back to the moment they walk through the door and remember something special coming in with them,’ he said.

    Heart of the film: Isabelle in Les Mis with Hugh Jackman who describes her as 'incredible, totally pure and unspolit'. 'She is the movie,' Jackman said

    Among stars: Isabelle in a scene with Helena Bonham Carter (left) and Sacha Baron Cohen. Jeremy James Taylor, founder of the National Youth Music Theatre said her star potential was obvious from the moment he met her

    He recalled meeting Redmayne, winner of a Laurence Olivier Award for Best Actor in a Supporting Role, as a freckle-faced 17-year-old. 

    ‘Even then he was a very good actor and a wonderful singer.’

    Little Britain’s Matt Lucas, who has also appeared on the stage in Les Mis, is another successful alumni.

    Taylor said: ‘Matt was about 13 when he first joined us. He was extremely funny.’ Flamboyant pop singer Mika also started out with Taylor. ‘We first came across him in a church opera. As a little boy he had one of the most beautiful  treble voices I’ve ever heard in my life,’ he said. ‘I saw him as a classical singer, but then he was always an unusual and mysterious chap.

    Perhaps his most famous former student is Jude Law. ‘He was about 13 and so beautiful that at first some people thought he was a girl,’ Taylor said.

    'She's incredible. Totally pure and unspoilt. She is the movie.'


    Hugh Jackman on Isabelle

    All of which puts Isabelle in a very select club. Just over a year ago she was an ordinary little girl who lived with her father Nigel, managing director of a  computer company, mother Elaine, an interior designer, and a 14-year-old sister Amelia, in East Sussex where she enjoyed playing the piano, singing, cycling and hanging out with her friends.

    YET last Wednesday the schoolgirl walked the red carpet at the glittering UK premier of Les Miserables in Leicester Square with a quiet assurance that belied her youth. ‘It’s been so exciting and so much fun, she said. ‘All the stars were so nice to me. When I first went on the set I was a bit scared, but really excited.’

    She quickly discovered, however, that acting is not always glamorous. 

    She had to walk barefoot for hours in cold fake snow, her eyes stung from the menthol used to make her cry on cue, and once she accidentally spilled a bucket of freezing water over herself.

    But if she has adapted with remarkable ease to her new status her parents remain stunned by her change of fortune. ‘We never expected her to win such a major part. It came totally out of the blue,’ says mother Elaine. 

    ‘It’s still very strange seeing her face on the poster. But she has taken it all in her stride and is very grounded. She’s still the same Issy.’

    World premiere: Isabelle with the cast on Wednesday where she said: 'All the stars were so nice to me. When I first went on the set I was a bit scared, but really excited'

    The schoolgirl has already discovered one of the pitfalls of fame, after someone set up a fake Twitter account in her name. A clearly confused Isabelle tweeted: ‘Why would someone pretend to be me??’

    Only a short time later she tweeted, more wisely: ‘I’m not really sure why  people are making up things about me. I don’t want to get involved in it tho.’

    Now, on the back of her film success, she has landed the same role in the current West End production of Les Miserables and film scripts are pouring in. 

    Her agent, Gaynor Shaw of Hobsons International, says Isabelle has what it takes to go far in the business. 

    ‘She is clever, vivacious and very funny. She certainly should have a very bright future as an actress.’

    Les Miserables is released in America on Christmas Day with its first showing in the UK on January 11.

    And our man's verdict? A fantastique five stars

    Rating:

    By Matthew Bond

    Four years ago, I took my then ten-year-old son to see Les Miserables in London’s West End for a birthday treat.

    To my relief and slight surprise, he loved it but what really stuck in my memory is the whispered conversation from the row behind at the Queen’s Theatre.

    ‘This is my eighth time,’ said one excited female voice, ‘can’t wait.’

    ‘It’s my 13th,’ said her neighbour.

    ‘I win,’ said a triumphant voice a little further along.

    ‘This is my 21st time and I’ve already booked the 22nd.’

    This, I quickly realised, was the extraordinary cultural phenomenon that is Les Miserables in action.

    Doomed: When Anne Hathaway, as poor, doomed Fantine, sings, in a high fragile, faltering voice, it will break your heart

    Critics can argue over whether it is Claude-Michel Schonberg’s rousing score, Herbert Kretzmer’s English-language lyrics or the original staging by Trevor Nunn and John Caird, but Les Mis, as it is known to its army of fans, is a musical that gets under the skin.

    You can go in not knowing a single note but you’ll come out singing at least five different songs. ‘Can you hear the people sing?’ asks the chorus repeatedly. Well, 60 million people in 42 different countries already have.

    Tom Hooper’s stunning new film adaptation can only add to the stunning – and somewhat surprising  – popularity of a musical that owes its origins to the 1862 novel of the same less-than-inspiring name, written by  Victor Hugo and set in the post-revolutionary France of the  mid-19th Century. It is a tale of disappointment, betrayal and death and yet audiences love it.

    In bringing it to the screen, Hooper, who in 2011 won the Oscar for Best Director for The King’s Speech, made two bold and quite brilliant decisions. Faced with a production in which all but perhaps a dozen lines of dialogue are sung rather than spoken, he decided to use actors rather than singers and then got them to sing ... for real, in each and every take. If there has been any clever tinkering in the dubbing suite afterwards, you certainly can’t hear it.

    The result is truly transformative. You go to see the stage musical because you want to hear the finest singing: you’ll go  to see the film because the raw emotions of Hugo’s tragedy-laced story shine through as never before. Susan Boyle brought a tear to the eye when she famously belted out I Dreamed A Dream on Britain’s Got Talent but when Anne Hathaway, as poor, doomed Fantine, sings it here, in a high, fragile, faltering voice, it will break your heart. Australian actor Hugh Jackman takes on the central role of Jean Valjean and does so so wonderfully that awards are surely certain to be showered upon him.

    Jackman is almost unrecognisable for the first 15 minutes when Valjean, a convict who has served 19 years for stealing a single loaf of bread, is finally released from prison. But, slowly, his handsome features emerge as the decades pass and, having broken the terms of his parole by taking on an assumed name, he becomes a successful businessman and  Mayor of Montreuil.

    Comedic input: Helena Bonham Carter as Madame Thenardier in the movie

    It is here that he meets the once beautiful Fantine, now working as a prostitute, taking on responsibility first for her and then in due course for her daughter, Cosette, played initially by Isabelle Allen and then later, as an adult, by Mamma Mia! actress Amanda Seyfried. But strive as Valjean might to be redeemed for his past sins, he runs the constant risk of being  un-masked by his arch-enemy, Inspector Javert, a policeman who never forgets a face.

    Russell Crowe plays Javert and does so with commendable courage. Like one or two other members of the cast, he doesn’t have the strongest singing voice but he’s in tune, and, most importantly, positively oozes the sort of malevolence that this crucial part requires.

    Hathaway’s commitment to what is very much a supporting part is even more impressive. As one of the most beautiful actresses in Hollywood, she sacrifices two of her crowning glories – her hair and her figure – to the role of Fantine. As the tears roll down her sunken cheeks, it is, of course, entirely right that her voice breaks with emotion. And, by the time this epic picture ends (be warned, it runs for almost 2 hours and 40 minutes) her cheeks won’t be the only damp ones, I can promise you that.

    Of course, it won’t be for everyone: musical theatre-cum-light opera never is. There may even be die-hard fans of Les Mis who take exception to their favourites being sung a little less tunefully than they are used to. But I’m convinced Hooper’s vital, visceral, mould-breaking production will win new fans.

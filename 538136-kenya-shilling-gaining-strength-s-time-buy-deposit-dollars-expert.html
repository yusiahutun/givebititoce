    • This followed the Central Bank of Kenya's (CBK) and the National Treasury's move to raise dollar reserves through the infrastructure bond (IFB) auction

    Commercial banks in the country quoted the dollar at around KSh 140 to KSh 147 (selling) and KSh 133 (buying) as of Thursday, February 15.

    According to market experts, the trend has been triggered by an influx of US currency from foreign investors and international lenders.

    Will the Kenya shilling lose again?

    In an exclusive interview with TUKO.co.ke, Moe, a market analyst at MoneyAcademyKE said once the infrastructure bond (IFB) auction closes on Monday, the shilling could shoot back to the KSh 160 mark.

    "From next week, it will start going up. Analysts have revised their expectations from 200 year end to 165 points."Foreign investors are selling dollars because they successfully bid on the IFB. Imagine right how billions of dollars are getting converted into Kenya shilling since the IFB is denominated in the local currency," Moe explained.

    What you should do to gain from Kenya shilling strength

    Moe recommended buying the US currency at the current rate of KSh 140 and saving in the USD account.

    "It is important to remember that Kenya is a net importer and the sustained demand for dollars puts pressure on the shillingâ€™s value," he noted.

    This means once the IFB auction closes, banks will still need more dollars to sell to importers of goods.

    The market analyst suggested the following ways to help investors get value from the appreciating shilling:

    • Buy US Dollars at the current low prices
    • Put them in a USD bank account
    • Invest in a USD MMF and wait

    Money market funds (MMFs) specialise in short-term, low-risk debt securities, providing investors with a stable option to save their money and achieve modest returns.

    What is mushroom coffee? 

    Mushroom coffee is exactly what its name suggests—it’s regular coffee mixed with functional mushrooms, which can contain adaptogens. These compounds are “nontoxic, non-specific, normalizing herbs and fungi that help our bodies adapt to stressors,” according to Dawson.

    What’s the difference between mushroom coffee and regular coffee?

    Per our expert, “Adaptogens help combat some of the negative side effects that often come with a cup of coffee, like an afternoon crash and jitters, so you get to keep your favorite coffee habit while leaving behind many of the negatives.”

    Is mushroom coffee good for you?

    Mushroom coffee’s adaptogenic benefits can improve your overall wellness, cognition, and immunity—all without causing you to crash or experience jittery feelings. So in short, yes, mushroom coffee can be good for you.

    What should you look for in a mushroom coffee?

    First and foremost, Dawson says, “Look for coffee that is coffee, meaning a base of real organic Arabica ground or whole bean coffee. You don’t have to switch to a coffee alternative or even an instant coffee if that isn’t your thing.” Check off this box and then look for coffee that uses real mushroom fruiting body extracts instead of mycelium to reap the most benefits. “Lastly, look for brands that are not only organic but third-party laboratory tested to ensure the highest quality, cleanest product possible,” Dawson adds.

    Who is our expert?

    Dawson is a registered herbalist (RH) of the American Herbalists Guild (AHG), certified holistic nutritionist, best-selling author, and head of education and innovation at Four Sigmatic. She received her BA in Environmental Studies and Philosophy from Whitman College and went on to study Ayurveda in India, becaming a certified yoga instructor on the banks of the Ganges River. Following that, she worked with a naturopathic doctor in Indonesia where she became a certified raw chef and detox coach.

    From India, she then made her way to Thailand, which is where her interest in herbal medicine really soared. After many years spent in Asia, Dawson journeyed back to the United States, landing in Boulder, Colorado to formalize her education in holistic medicine. After receiving her graduate degree at the Colorado School of Clinical Herbalism (CSCH), she opened her private practice where she worked with hundreds of clients specializing in a functional mushroom-based treatment for individuals with autoimmune conditions and chronic illness. Dawson additionally served as an instructor of mycology at CSCH for five years.

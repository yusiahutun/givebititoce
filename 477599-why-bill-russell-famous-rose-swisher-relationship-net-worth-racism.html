    Early life

    Bill Russell (Real name William Felton Russell) was born in Monroe, Louisiana, USA, on February 12, 1934. He was the younger son of Charles Russell and Katie Russell. He had an older brother, Charlie Louis Russell, Jr., an American writer best known for his play Fie on the Black Hand Side.

    Russell spent his early life in Monroe, a small agricultural community heavily segregated along racial lines. His parents wanted to bring up their children in a better environment. As a result, his family moved and settled in Oakland, California. But while Oakland was better than Louisiana, the financial situation was not, and the family lived in poverty.

    Things worsened when Russell lost his mother at just 12 years due to kidney failure, leaving him and his brother to be raised by their father.

    Bill Russell attended McClymonds High in Oakland, California. He had tremendous athletic potential being tall, a good runner, and a jumper. Unfortunately, he had a hard time understanding the game of basketball. His high school coach encouraged him to learn the basics, and he was an excellent player by his senior year.

    After high school, he received a scholarship to the University of San Francisco (USF), where he played for the varsity basketball team. He was a star player during his college years, famous for his defensive play and shot blocking, which even forced the NCAA to institute a basketball interference rule.

    Career

    Russell started his professional basketball career by representing the U.S. men’s basketball team in the 1956 Summer Olympics in Melbourne, Australia. He was named the team’s captain, leading them to a gold medal win against the Soviet Union in the final game.

    NBA career

    The personality joined the NBA during the 1956 draft as the second overall pick by St. Louis Hawks. He was then traded to Bolton Celtics, joining them for the 1956/57 season. For his rookie year, he played 48 games with an average of 19.6 rebounds and 14.7 points. His addition to the Celtics roster helped boost the already strong team.

    Over the years, Bill Russell became a legend in basketball and is considered one of the most successful players in the history of the NBA. He won 11 championships in the 13 seasons he was with the Celtics. In addition, he earned five regular season NBA MVP awards and was selected to the all-NBA First Teams three times.

    Russell set the record for being the first player to average 20 rebounds per game for an entire season. He is also famous for being the second player to make 51 rebounds in a single game. He finished his basketball career in the 1967/68 season when he was 35. His prowess on the court had slowed, but he still managed an impressive average of 18.6 rebounds and 12.5 points.

    Fight against racism

    Russell’s life was marred by incidences of racism from when he was a child in Louisiana to his college and even NBA career. For example, during the 1954 All-College Tournament, Russell and his fellow black players at USF were denied hotel accommodation in Oklahoma City for being black.

    He was also subjected to racial abuse and discrimination when playing for Bolton Celtics, including harassment from overzealous Celtics fans. Other instances include the 1958 off-season NBA-ALL-Stars tour, where Russell and other black teammates were denied hotel accommodation in North Carolina.

    A similar scenario occurred in the 1961/62 exhibition game, where he and other black teammates were refused service in a restaurant in Lexington, Kentucky. Despite the racial injustice, he never allowed himself to become a victim. Instead, he fought against societal injustices and was unafraid to stand up for what he believed was right.
